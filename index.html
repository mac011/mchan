Albedo

Interactive Albedo Exhibit

Summer Engineering Experience with EnVision Maker Studio of Jacobs School of Engineering.

Worked with Birch Aquarium at Scripps Institution of Oceanography to deliver an interactive exhibit that educates users and inspires conservation efforts in a fun and engaging manner.

What is Albedo?

Discover

In our needfinding process, we observed visitor behaviors, conducted interviews and research. We discovered that the most popular attractions were robust, while providing physical feedback. However, it was also clear that some kids did not receive the educational message that was meant to support the exhibit. Our clients also required the exhibit to retain its scientific accuracy. Finally, we came up with the following mission statment:

Mission: To engineer an educational Albedo exhibit that physically responds to user interaction, while maintaining scientific accuracy.

Ideate

In the ideating process, we generated 3 different concepts of a tabletop, globe, and room design. We discussed the pros and cons of each deisgn, while considering feasibility. Due to the nature of our light sensors, we decided to settle with the tabletop design.

Prototype

For our prototype, we used two light sensors. One top sensor to measure the incoming light, and one bottom sensor to measure the reflected light. By taking the ratio of the reflected to incoming light, we were able to digitally display an albedo value.

Test

In order to ensure scientific accuracy, we conducted multiple tests alongside the real pyranometers to compare our albedo values to the real albedo values used for research.

Product

Our deliverable is an air hockey tabletop design that features changing albedo values in response to float icebergs that pass under the sensors.

This is a picture of our custom PCP boards which house the top and bottom light sensors.

This is the center mount that houses the hardware for the sensors and visual feedback. Local albedo values correspond to each sensor, and the average of all 4 of these local albedo values contribute to the global albedo value.

As floating icebergs pass under the sensors to change the albedo values, the LED lights around the table also provide visual feedback to the users. As albedo is lowered, the lights turn red to reflect a heating effect. As albedo rises, the lights turn blue to show a cooling effect.

Spotify Redesign

Redesigning Spotify's playlist creation and song deletion features.

Needfinding

In our journey to redesign Spotify, we tested users to discover needs. We asked our users when the last time they created a playlist was and gave our users the basic instructions to create that new playlist as a demo. We also asked them to add and delete songs to this newly created playlist. The users were asked to repeat this same procedure with a similar music app. Through our observations and needfinding, we compiled the following list of scenarios:

Create Playlist
  • Create new playlist from scratch
  • Create new playlist with multiple songs
  • Create new playlist for running

    • Manage Playlist
  • Add/ delete 2 songs from the playlist
  • Rename the playlist
  • Rearrange the song order in the playlist

    • Other
  • Find all available songs by an artist
  • Preview a song
  • Add song from one playlist to another


    • Competitive Comparisons

    We then compared features across different apps and found instances where others apps like Apple Music, structured a feature well versus a parallel feature that was less good.





    Competitive Analysis : Information Architecture

    We also compared the information architecture of Spotify, Apple Music, and Soundcloud. All three of the apps had 5 main navigation buttons that overlapped a bit across apps.

    Spotify was unique in that it had genres and moods in the Home. The third level also showed that Spotify's interface utilized graphics more than just text.


    Apple's For You seemed to have the most emphasis. It is neatly organized into a grid format and has an intuitive interface.


    Most of the bustle is condensed onto the Home page of Soundcloud.

    Prototypes

    Creating a Workout Playlist:

    Finally, we were able to proceed to redesigning Spotify. We made specific changes to the creation of a playlist and the deletion of songs. We believe the changes we propose will allow for better user experience.


    Our prototype is more user friendly when creating a playlist because it allows users to dynamically find new music and simply click the plus button next to the song/ album they desire to add it onto their playlist.

    Although Spotify has song suggestions at the bottom of a playlist for users to easily add on to the current playlist, it does not allow the user to quickly add songs beyond these suggestions. For people to find more songs to add on to the playlist, they have to do it manually: they must move away from the current playlist in question to search/ browse for songs, tap on the ellipses of the selected song, tap "add to playlist," and finally add the new song onto the playlist.

    Our design for our prototype was inspired by Apple Music's "add music mode." Users can easily add songs anytime to a current playlist by clicking on the "Add songs" button that is statically on top of the navigation tabs in each playlist. It then takes the user to an adding songs mode where they can search songs, discover songs through popular playlists, or even just see the last few songs they listened to. In this mode, each album and song has a plus button next to it that turns into a check mark if selected and is added onto the current playlist.

    Song Deletion:

    In terms of deleting songs in a playlist, our app does this well because of the multiple ways that it allows users to delete a song.

    In Spotify, users are limited because they can only delete songs when they click on the three dots at the top of the playlist and click "edit," which then gives the option to delete the songs on the playlist.

    There are three ways to delete songs in our app. First of all, to cater to advanced users, swiping left on a particular song will quickly remove it from the playlist. This design is directly inspired by Spotify, except in Spotify, this option is only possible when the song is added to the library (not necessarily the current playlist); so for our app, we make it possible for any of the songs on a playlist and it deletes it from that playlist it is currently in. Another way to delete a song is to click on the three dots next to a song, where the options for that song come up, one of which is the option to delete it. And finally, another way to delete a song (or even multiple songs) is to go into edit mode on the playlist. From the edit mode, the buttons next to the songs change and become circles that can be tapped to select and delete multiple songs at once. After selecting the desired song(s) in edit mode, the user can then hit the "delete" button at the bottom.
